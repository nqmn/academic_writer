# Q1 WoS Readiness Standard

This module defines the default quality bar for manuscripts, manuscript sections, literature reviews, and review-oriented outputs produced by the Scholarly Research Writing and Review Agent System.

## Core Principle

All outputs should aim to meet a strong Q1 Web of Science journal standard unless the user explicitly requests a lower bar, a different genre, or an early-stage draft.

This standard requires:

- meaningful novelty
- rigorous and transparent methodology
- strong theoretical grounding or conceptual justification
- international relevance where appropriate
- disciplined academic English
- clear and defensible contribution claims

## Global Quality Gate

Before finalizing any substantial academic output, the agent should check:

1. Is the contribution specific rather than generic?
2. Is the gap evidenced rather than asserted?
3. Is the literature treated critically rather than descriptively?
4. Is the methodology justified and replicable, where relevant?
5. Are the implications proportional to the evidence?
6. Is the prose publishable in tone, structure, and precision?

If any answer is weak, the agent should revise toward Q1-level rigor.

## Section Standards

### 1. Title and research focus

The title should be:

- specific
- concise
- keyword-relevant
- aligned with the actual contribution

Avoid:

- vague topic labels
- inflated novelty terms
- excessively broad scope claims

### 2. Abstract

The abstract should include:

- background or problem
- gap
- objective
- method
- key findings
- contribution
- implication

Avoid:

- topic-only summaries
- vague claims about findings
- missing novelty or quantitative signal where relevant

### 3. Introduction

The introduction should:

- establish a real global or field-level problem context
- demonstrate the gap using current literature
- justify why the problem matters
- state the objective clearly
- explain what is novel
- state the contribution explicitly

The introduction must answer:

- why the issue matters
- what prior work has not resolved
- why the present approach is different or stronger
- why the journal audience should care

### 4. Literature review

The literature review should:

- use recent high-quality sources
- synthesize rather than list studies
- compare methods, findings, and assumptions
- identify contradictions and unresolved issues
- support a framework, hypotheses, or research direction

Avoid:

- descriptive summaries with no analytical thread
- weak theoretical framing
- outdated citation patterns unless seminal

### 5. Theoretical framework or hypothesis logic

Where applicable, the work should:

- use established theory, model logic, or conceptual framing
- define variables clearly
- justify hypothesis paths or conceptual links
- show extension, integration, or refinement of theory when possible

Avoid:

- unsupported hypotheses
- weak or absent theoretical justification

### 6. Methodology

The methodology should be:

- clearly designed
- justified
- transparent
- replicable where possible

Minimum expectations:

- research design
- sampling or data-source logic
- data-collection clarity
- analytical procedure
- validity or reliability treatment where relevant
- ethical consideration where relevant

### 7. Results

Results should:

- directly answer the stated objectives or hypotheses
- present evidence clearly
- avoid duplication across text and tables
- interpret statistics accurately without overclaiming

Avoid:

- p-value dumping without interpretation
- disconnected result reporting

### 8. Discussion

The discussion should:

- explain why the findings matter
- connect findings back to theory
- compare with prior literature
- explain contradictions where relevant
- identify what new understanding emerges

Avoid:

- repeating results without interpretation
- generic implications

### 9. Contributions

The contribution statement should distinguish:

- theoretical contribution
- methodological contribution
- empirical contribution
- practical or policy contribution

Avoid:

- generic claims such as "this study contributes to the literature" without specifying how

### 10. Conclusion

The conclusion should:

- answer the objective
- restate the contribution
- acknowledge limitations
- suggest future work proportionately

Avoid:

- abstract repetition
- overclaiming

### 11. References

The reference base should:

- rely primarily on strong indexed journals where possible
- include current literature, especially from the last five years
- retain seminal sources where necessary
- remain consistent in formatting

### 12. Language and academic writing

The prose should be:

- formal
- concise
- coherent
- precise
- free of redundancy

The agent should assume that Q1-level writing requires near publication-ready English.

### 13. Figures, tables, and visuals

Where relevant, visuals should be:

- readable
- labeled clearly
- consistent
- interpretation-supporting rather than decorative

### 14. Ethical and publication standards

Where relevant, the manuscript should state or account for:

- ethical approval
- informed consent
- funding
- conflict of interest
- data availability
- AI-use disclosure if required by the venue

## Novelty Standard

Strong Q1-level novelty usually involves one or more of:

- an important unresolved problem
- a genuine methodological advance
- a theoretical extension or integration
- a stronger empirical basis
- a new dataset, context, or cross-context comparison
- an interdisciplinary contribution
- meaningful policy or industry implications

Novelty claims must remain evidence-based and proportional.

## Common Rejection Risks

Treat the following as red flags:

- weak novelty
- unsupported gap statements
- descriptive literature review only
- thin theoretical grounding
- weak or under-justified methods
- local relevance with no broader significance
- poor English
- shallow discussion
- generic implications

## Readiness Scorecard

Use this internal checklist before presenting a manuscript-level output:

- strong novelty
- clear research gap
- high-quality literature review
- strong theoretical foundation
- rigorous methodology
- sufficiently advanced analysis
- deep discussion
- significant contribution
- international relevance where appropriate
- strong references
- professional English
- ethics and publication compliance

Interpretation:

- 10 to 12 strong -> strong Q1 potential
- 7 to 9 strong -> likely major revision territory
- below 7 strong -> high rejection risk

## Usage Rule

Apply this module by default to:

- manuscript drafting
- section drafting
- literature-review synthesis
- research-gap positioning
- manuscript revision and upgrading

It may be relaxed only when the user explicitly asks for:

- an early rough draft
- a lower-tier venue target
- brainstorming notes rather than submission-grade prose
