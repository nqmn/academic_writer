# Theoretical Framework Methodology

## Purpose

Use this module when the task involves:

- selecting a theoretical or conceptual framework for a study
- articulating how a framework connects to the research question, methods, and analysis
- writing or reviewing a theoretical framework section
- framing theoretical contributions or evaluating novelty claims
- constructing a literature review organised around theoretical or conceptual concepts

Load this module together with `methodology/gap_validation.md` and `rhetorical_moves/contribution_framing.md` whenever theoretical positioning is central to the task.

## Definitions

### Theory (Lim, 2026)

A theory is a lens to comprehend, explain, and predict a phenomenon. It involves:

- Abstraction (what, where, when, who): defines context and scope
- Assumption (why, how): provides rationale for mechanisms and relationships

A good theory is conceptualizable (explainable), operationalizable (testable), generalizable, extendible, and falsifiable.

### Theoretical Framework (Grant & Osanloo, 2014; Luft et al., 2022)

The theoretical framework is the lens through which the researcher views the phenomenon. It:

- is derived from existing, tested theory
- reveals the researcher's assumptions and subjectivities
- shapes research questions, data collection, and analysis
- is not borrowed wholesale but selected and justified

### Conceptual Framework (Luft et al., 2022)

The conceptual framework describes the researcher's understanding of the factors or variables involved and their presumed relationships. It:

- is constructed by the researcher, not taken wholesale from literature
- may include established theory and emergent ideas
- must describe relationships among concepts, not merely list them
- guides both data collection and analysis

A flowchart of the research design is not a conceptual framework.

## Selecting a Theoretical Framework

### Three Key Considerations (Luft et al., 2022)

1. Reflect on the phenomenon and the researcher's assumptions — the assumptions should align with the framework.
2. Select a framework that connects clearly to all parts of the study: literature review, data collection, analysis, and discussion.
3. Consider the orientation: individual-level, institutional-level, or policy-level.

### Eight-Step Selection Process (Grant & Osanloo, 2014)

1. Identify your epistemological worldview.
2. Consider several theories that intersect with that worldview.
3. Develop working knowledge of those theories.
4. Conduct a brief literature review to find theoretical support.
5. Consult prior dissertation applications of the framework.
6. Consider arguments that oppose your theoretical assumptions.
7. Apply answers to how the theory connects to the problem, purpose, significance, and design.
8. Select one framework that provides a coherent blueprint for the study.

### Integration Checklist (Grant & Osanloo, 2014)

Before finalising framework choice:

- Which discipline will the theory be applied to?
- Is the theory an appropriate fit with the methodological plan?
- Does the methodology draw from the principles and tenets of the framework?
- How big is the theory — are specific concepts selected to meet study objectives?
- Do the problem, purpose, and significance align with the framework?
- Can the theory be used with the research questions, or do the RQs need modification?
- How does the framework inform the literature review?
- Does the data analysis plan use a priori codes from the framework, or a posteriori codes for an emergent framework?
- Does the framework undergird conclusions, implications, and recommendations?

## Typology of Theory (Lim, 2026)

| Type | Description |
|------|-------------|
| Concept theory | Theory of a single concept |
| Relationship theory | Theory of direct relationships between concepts |
| Typology theory | Theory classifying multiple concepts into categories |
| Framework (model) theory | Theory addressing multiple relationships including mediation and moderation |
| Integrated (hybrid) theory | Combines multiple frameworks |

Identify the type of theory being used or developed before writing the theoretical framework section.

---

## Theoretical Foundations: Five Questions (Lim, 2026)

Use these questions to structure the theoretical foundations section or the theoretical background discussion:

1. What is (are) the focal theory(ies)?
2. Why is (are) the focal theory(ies) important?
3. How have existing studies used the focal theory(ies)?
4. How is the present study using the focal theory(ies)?
5. How is the present study contributing back to the focal theory(ies)?

## Theoretical Gaps: The 3Us (Lim, 2026)

When the task involves identifying a theoretical gap, classify it first:

- Unexplored: no research attention has been given to this aspect.
- Underdeveloped: recognised but insufficiently developed theoretically or empirically.
- Uncertain: conflicting or inconsistent findings remain unresolved.

After classifying, attach a problematization:

- Debates: contested assumptions.
- Disconnects: gaps between theory and practice.
- Direness: urgency from repeated neglect.

## Constructing a Conceptual Framework

Key principles (Luft et al., 2022):

1. The framework is constructed by the researcher — it is not borrowed wholesale.
2. Draw on literature, personal experience as student or instructor, and thought experiments.
3. Must describe relationships among the components, not simply list them.
4. Relationships should guide both data collection and analysis.
5. Should address gaps or contradictions discovered in the literature review.

---

## Using a Concept Matrix to Organise the Literature Review (Klopper et al., 2007)

When building the literature review that supports a theoretical framework:

1. Formulate a clear problem statement and extract key concepts from it.
2. Use those concepts as column headers in a concept matrix.
3. Survey abstracts and summaries to identify relevant references.
4. Tick cells where a concept appears in a reference.
5. Conduct critical comparative analysis across all references under each concept column.
6. Write the literature review using the concept columns as section headings.

The concept matrix prevents patchwork reviewing and ensures that the literature review is structured around the same conceptual categories that appear in the theoretical framework.
