# Literature Reviews, Theoretical Frameworks, and Conceptual Frameworks: An Introduction for New Biology Education Researchers

**Citation:** Luft, J. A., Jeong, S., Idsardi, R., & Gardner, G. (2022). Literature reviews, theoretical frameworks, and conceptual frameworks: An introduction for new biology education researchers. *CBE—Life Sciences Education, 21*(rm1), 1–10. https://doi.org/10.1187/cbe.21-05-0134

**Keywords:** Literature review, theoretical framework, conceptual framework, biology education research, DBER

---

## Core Argument

Literature reviews, theoretical frameworks, and conceptual frameworks are three distinct but intertwined elements essential to educational research. Their purpose and function are frequently confused by new researchers. When aligned, they make a study coherent and ensure findings contribute to the field; when disconnected, the study suffers.

> Fewer than 25% of research articles in CBE-LSE (2015–2019) had a theoretical or conceptual framework.

---

## Overview Comparison Table

| Element | Purpose | Aims | Connection to Manuscript | Additional Points |
|---------|---------|------|--------------------------|------------------|
| **Literature review** | Point out the need for the study and its connection to the field | Comprehensive, critical, purposeful examination of current/relevant research | Connects to study question, guides methodology, central to discussion | May reach beyond BER to other education research fields |
| **Theoretical framework** | State assumptions and orientations of the researcher regarding the topic | Illuminates the phenomenon and corresponding researcher assumptions; can take different orientations | Drives the question, guides data collection/analysis methods, informs findings discussion, reveals researcher subjectivities | Does not rationalize the need for the study; can come from different fields |
| **Conceptual framework** | Describe the researcher's understanding of the main concepts under investigation | Created by researcher; includes presumed relationships among concepts; addresses needed areas from literature review | Informed by literature reviews, experiences, or experiments; may include emergent ideas; must be coherent with theoretical framing | Articulates the phenomenon through written descriptions and/or visual representations |

## Literature Reviews

### Purpose
A well-conducted review:
- Summarizes existing research on a specific topic
- Identifies unanswered questions
- Illustrates the current project's contribution to the field
- Justifies the methodological approach

### Building a Literature Review

**"Scholars before researchers"** (Boote & Beile, 2005) — develop working knowledge before conducting research.

**Practical tips:**
1. Create a "summary phrase" or "research nugget" — a brief descriptive statement about the study focus (e.g., "first-year nonmajor students' understanding of evolution")
2. Use the phrase to identify 2+ review areas
3. Start with literature from the last 5 years; also read seminal works
4. Read until conclusions start sounding similar — that signals sufficient coverage
5. Let the review substantiate the methodological approach

**Scope considerations:**
- Not too narrow (no real research question emerges) or too broad (no clear focus)
- BER studies may need literature from outside biology (DBER, general education research)
- Emerging topics may need transdisciplinary reading

**Evaluating study quality** (per AERA standards):
- Adequate review of research informing the study
- Sound and appropriate data collection and analysis methods
- Conclusions that do not overstep or underexplore the analyzed data
- Exclude fatally flawed studies (data irrelevant to the question, inadequate analysis, poorly constructed instruments)

### Placement in Articles
- **Within the introduction**: literature review follows opening paragraphs, clarifies the need for the study
- **As a separate section**: distinct subsections covering different aspects of the topic

## Theoretical Frameworks

### Purpose and Analogy

Like scientists observing the same event (a fog tsunami) through different disciplinary lenses, researchers approach the same phenomenon through different theoretical frameworks. Each framework:
- Provides a particular perspective or "lens"
- Shapes the types of questions asked
- Guides data collection and analysis methods
- Informs discussion of results
- Reveals the researcher's subjectivities (values, social experience, viewpoint)

> All research questions arise from the researcher's implicit or explicit assumptions of a phenomenon.

### Selecting a Theoretical Framework: Three Key Considerations

**1. Reflect on the phenomenon and assumptions**
- What is the studied event (student learning, instructional approach, group organization)?
- What assumptions does the researcher hold about how it will be affected?
- The researcher's assumptions about the phenomenon align with a theoretical framework

*Example*: A researcher who assumes more knowledgeable students support group learning will align with Vygotsky's (1978) sociocultural theory.

**2. Select a framework that clearly connects to all parts of the study**
- Must augment the study with a perspective providing greater insights
- Must align with studies described in the literature review
- Data collection and analysis methods must correspond to the framework
- Framework should provide added meaning to findings, and findings should align with the framework

**3. Consider the orientation of the framework**
- **Individual-level** frameworks: understand actions, learning, or knowledge of individuals
- **Institutional-level** frameworks: insights into how institutions/organizations/groups influence individuals
- **Policy-level** frameworks: how national or local policies dictate outcomes or instructional design

### Example: Jensen & Lawson (2011) — Vygotsky's Sociocultural Theory
- Phenomenon: student learning through peer interactions in undergraduate biology
- Assumption: more knowledgeable peers are important; learning is social
- Framework: Vygotsky (1978) sociocultural theory of learning and development
- Data collected: instruction type, group composition, reasoning abilities, achievement
- Finding extension: group composition had conditional effects — heterogeneous groups benefited from traditional instruction while homogeneous low-reasoning groups benefited from inquiry-based interaction

## Conceptual Frameworks

### Purpose
A conceptual framework describes:
- The way a researcher understands the **factors/variables** involved in the study
- The **presumed relationships** among those concepts
- Concepts articulated using **relevant literature** (and sometimes emergent, not-yet-established ideas)

### Distinguishing Conceptual from Theoretical Frameworks

| | Theoretical Framework | Conceptual Framework |
|-|-----------------------|----------------------|
| Breadth | Narrower; established theory | Broader; includes established theory + emergent ideas |
| Source | Taken from established literature | Constructed by the researcher |
| Grounding | Well-tested, peer-reviewed | May include ideas not yet formally tested |
| Nature | Articulates the lens | More mechanistic and malleable |

Conceptual frameworks **encompass** theoretical frameworks and add the researcher's own emergent understanding.

### Constructing a Conceptual Framework

Key principles:
1. Conceptual frameworks are **constructed by the researchers** — they are not borrowed wholesale
2. Draw on literature, but also on experiences as student/instructor and thought experiments
3. Must describe **relationships** among components of the investigation
4. Relationships should **guide methods** and inform both data collection and analysis
5. Should **address gaps or contradictions** discovered in the literature review

**Important distinction**: A flowchart of the research design ≠ a conceptual framework. A model depicting study design does not serve the same role as articulating conceptual relationships.

### Example: Sabel et al. (2017) — Scaffolds and Metacognition
- Integrated two theoretical frameworks: self-regulated learning + metacognition
- Conceptual framework showed how external scaffolds (enhanced answer keys, reflection questions) interact with internal factors to support student learning
- The framework provided rationale for focusing on two specific scaffolds and not other variables (self-efficacy, exam format, etc.)

### When Researchers Draw on Others' Conceptual Frameworks
If the researcher's conceptualization closely aligns with an existing framework, a brief mention with additional citations is sufficient (e.g., Ghee et al., 2016 used portions of SCCT as their conceptual framework).

---

## Key Takeaways

1. These three elements are **intertwined** — misalignment among them weakens the entire study
2. **Literature review** → justifies the study and guides methodology
3. **Theoretical framework** → not arbitrary; reveals researcher subjectivities and shapes inquiry
4. **Conceptual framework** → constructed by the researcher; articulates presumed relationships explicitly
5. There is **constant back-and-forth** among these elements as a study evolves
6. Being explicit about all three gives readers confidence in the rigor and coherence of the study
