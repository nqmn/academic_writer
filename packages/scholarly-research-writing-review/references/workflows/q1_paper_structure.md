# Q1 Paper Structure and Templates

This file provides structural templates, formulas, and section-order conventions for Q1 WoS manuscripts. It is complementary to `q1_wos_readiness.md`, which covers quality standards, reviewer weights, and rejection risks.

Source guidance integrated from: Busse C & August E (2020) "How to Write and Publish a Research Paper for a Peer-Reviewed Journal." *Journal of Cancer Education*. https://doi.org/10.1007/s13187-020-01751-z

---

## Canonical Section Order

```
Title
Abstract
Keywords
1. Introduction
2. Literature Review
3. Theoretical Framework and Hypotheses
4. Methodology
5. Results
6. Discussion
7. Implications
8. Conclusion
9. Limitations
10. Future Research
References
Appendices
```

---

## Pre-Writing: Select a Target Journal First

Before drafting, identify the target journal. Each journal has a defined readership; the entire manuscript — background depth, terminology, framing — must be calibrated to that readership. Check the journal's "Aims and Scope" page. Locate peer-reviewer guidelines if available.

Do not submit to more than one journal simultaneously.

---

## Pre-Writing: Establish Author Roles

Agree in writing on author order, section ownership, corresponding author, and abstract/cover-letter responsibilities before drafting begins. Use ICMJE criteria for authorship eligibility. If multiple papers will result from a single study, assign lead authors and deadlines for each paper at the outset.

## Title Formula

```
[Core Variable or Innovation] + [Method or Approach] + [Context or Impact]
```

Examples:

- "Explainable AI Framework for Sustainable Smart Manufacturing: A Deep Learning Perspective"
- "Blockchain Adoption and Supply Chain Resilience: Evidence from Emerging Economies"
- "Machine Learning-Based Predictive Healthcare Analytics for Early Disease Detection"

### Title Writing Rules (Busse & August)

- Write the title **after** finishing the paper; the focus often shifts during drafting.
- The title should invite readers to continue past the first page: make it informative and specific.
- Consider including: independent variable, dependent variable, population, setting, study design, timing, and even the main result.
- Keywords must complement the title to maximise database discoverability.
- Avoid abbreviations unless universally recognised (e.g., SNP), because readers search using full terms.

---

## Introduction Structure (Upside-Down Triangle)

The introduction narrows from broad context to specific aim. Target length: three to five paragraphs. Include all five elements in order:

```
1. Why this research is important — the "So what?" context
2. What is already known about the topic (background)
3. The gap — what is NOT yet known
4. Why it is important to learn this new information
5. The specific research aim(s) that address the gap
```

The research aim must directly address the gap identified in element 3.

### Common Introduction Pitfalls

| Pitfall | Fix |
|---------|-----|
| Too generic; not written to the target journal's readers | Investigate the journal's readership; calibrate background depth accordingly |
| Citations inadequate to support claims | Every challengeable claim must carry one or more citations |
| Research aim is vague | The aim must contain setting, population/sample, study design, timing, dependent variable, and independent variables — enough for a reader to imagine the analysis |

## Methods Section Elements

The methods section must enable replication and give enough context to interpret results. Include all of the following:

```
1. Setting and participants
2. Study design and timing
3. Recruitment and sampling approach (with justification)
4. Data collection process
5. Dataset description
6. Dependent and independent variables
7. Covariates (with justification for inclusion)
8. Analytic approach for each research objective
9. Ethical approval
```

**Hallmark of an exemplary methods section:** every choice is justified, not merely described. State what you did, then defend why.

### Common Methods Pitfalls

| Pitfall | Fix |
|---------|-----|
| Methods described for only one aim, or only part of an aim | Verify methods cover all aims stated in the paper |
| No justification for choices made | Explicitly justify sampling approach, variable selection, and statistical procedures |

## Results Section Rules

Results must answer each part of the research aim. Focus on **associations**, not statistical tests.

### Sequence

```
1. Sample description — n enrolled, approached, eligible, and dropped out
2. Participant characteristics
3. Findings ordered by aim or by importance
```

### Key Rules

- Report non-significant associations. They are findings.
- Each table or figure must carry a clear standalone message; it should complement, not repeat, the text.
- Table/figure titles, legends, or footnotes must include sample, timing, comparisons, and test information.
- Prefer figures over tables where both are feasible.
- Define abbreviations in footnotes; avoid them in titles.
- No citations in the results section.
- Do not interpret results here.

### Common Results Pitfalls

| Pitfall | Fix |
|---------|-----|
| Text focuses on test statistics rather than associations | Report the relationship, not just the number: "women with exposure X were 3.4 times more likely to have disease Y" |
| Causal language used without causal design | Use "associated with" or "related to" unless the design justifies causal inference |
| Direction of association unclear | Be explicit: "an increase in X is associated with a decrease in Y" |

## Discussion Structure (Right-Side-Up Triangle)

The discussion widens from specific findings to general implications:

```
1. Mini synopsis — one or two sentences restating the main findings
2. Significance and meaning — lift the reader's gaze from the specific to the general
3. Compare results — agreement or disagreement with prior studies
4. Pathways — mechanisms that might explain the results
5. Strengths and limitations — candidly reported with implications described
6. Impact and applications — interventions, clinical protocols, policy
7. Suggestions for future work — specific, not generic
```

Avoid "future research is necessary." Instead name: the population underrepresented, the moderating variable uninvestigated, the context that limits generalisability.

### Common Discussion Pitfalls

| Pitfall | Fix |
|---------|-----|
| Detailed results repeated or new results presented | Focus on how findings fit the scientific literature; do not re-report specific values |
| Limitation implications not described | For each limitation, state how it affects the application of the findings |
| Future research statements too generic | Specify which relationship, which population, which variable, which context needs further study |

## Abstract Template

```
This study investigates [topic/problem] in the context of [industry/country/domain].
Despite growing attention to [topic], limited studies have explored [specific gap].

To address this gap, this study proposes/examines/develops [model/framework].
Using [methodology] with data collected from [sample/source], the study applies [analysis method].

The findings reveal that [main results].
Additionally, [secondary findings].

This study contributes to the literature by [theoretical contribution].
Practically, the findings provide insights for [industry/policy/managers].

The study advances understanding of [research area] and offers directions for future research.
```

---

## Problem Statement Formula

```
Although previous studies have investigated [topic],
limited attention has been given to [specific issue],
particularly in [context].

Existing findings remain inconsistent regarding [issue],
creating uncertainty in both theory and practice.

Therefore, a comprehensive investigation is needed to address [gap].
```

---

## Discussion Formula

```
The findings suggest that [result].
This supports/challenges previous studies by [authors].

One possible explanation is [reasoning].
The findings further extend [theory/framework] by demonstrating [contribution].
```

---

## Future Research Paragraph Template

```
Future studies may extend this work by employing longitudinal designs
to better capture causal relationships over time.

Cross-country comparative investigations may further improve
the generalizability of the findings.

Additionally, future research could integrate moderating variables
such as organizational culture and digital maturity.
```

## Reviewer Expectations by Section

| Section      | Reviewer main focus    |
|--------------|------------------------|
| Abstract     | Novelty and clarity    |
| Introduction | Research gap           |
| Literature   | Critical synthesis     |
| Methodology  | Rigor and validity     |
| Results      | Accuracy               |
| Discussion   | Contribution           |
| Conclusion   | Impact                 |
| Future Work  | Scholarly direction    |

## Manuscript Quality Checklist (Busse & August, Fig. 3)

Use this before finalising any substantial manuscript output:

- [ ] Written to the specific readers of the target journal
- [ ] Introduction follows the upside-down triangle (context → gap → aim)
- [ ] Research aim is specific and informative (includes setting, population, design, timing, variables)
- [ ] All dependent variables, independent variables, and covariates are thoroughly described in methods and results
- [ ] Each methodological choice is justified (sampling, variables, measurement, statistical approach)
- [ ] Results describe direction of associations, not only test statistics
- [ ] Table titles and figure legends are complete and standalone
- [ ] Results text summarises key findings from tables and figures rather than repeating them exactly
- [ ] Discussion does not repeat detailed results and introduces no new results
- [ ] Limitations are stated with their implications for application of the findings
- [ ] Future research suggestions are specific, not generic
- [ ] Aims, methods, results, and discussion are internally consistent — all stated aims are addressed throughout
- [ ] Title is informative and interesting
- [ ] Citations are appropriate and complete throughout
