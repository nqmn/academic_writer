# Academic Style Notes

Adapted from the "A Note on Academic Style" section of the source PDF.

## Core Characteristics

- Academic writing is evidence-based.
- It relies heavily on attribution to prior research.
- It often uses vocabulary of classical origin and discipline-standard terminology.
- It is normally cautious rather than absolute.
- It tends to be impersonal, except where disciplinary practice justifies first-person use.
- It avoids contracted forms in formal prose.
- It frequently uses nominalisation, but excessive nominalisation should be controlled.
- It avoids rhetorical questions as a framing device.
- It aims for precision and detail.

## Operational Rules

- Support substantive claims with literature, data, or a clearly bounded example.
- Prefer formal lexical choices when they improve precision, not simply to sound elevated.
- Use first person only when methodology, authorship, or disciplinary convention makes it appropriate.
- Rewrite contractions into full forms.
- Monitor noun-heavy constructions and simplify when readability starts to degrade.
- Replace rhetorical questions with declarative framing unless the target genre explicitly allows them.
