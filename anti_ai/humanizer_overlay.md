# Humanizer Overlay for Academic Writing

Adapted from `C:\Users\Intel\Downloads\humanizer.skill` and filtered for formal academic use.

## Purpose

- Reduce visible signs of AI-generated prose without collapsing into casual or conversational writing.
- Preserve academic caution, evidential discipline, and section-appropriate tone.
- Improve naturalness by removing formulaic emphasis, vague generality, and templated cadence.

## Do Not Import Uncritically

- Do not inject personality, humour, or first-person voice unless the genre explicitly permits it.
- Do not replace legitimate academic hedging with overconfident prose.
- Do not humanise by making claims less precise.

## High-Risk AI Markers

### 1. Significance inflation

Watch for:

- "pivotal moment"
- "transformative potential"
- "underscores the importance"
- "vital role"
- "broader landscape"
- "marks a shift"

Preferred correction:

- Replace inflated significance claims with concrete importance, mechanism, or scope.
- State what the phenomenon does, changes, or affects.

### 2. Superficial `-ing` chains

Watch for:

- "highlighting"
- "underscoring"
- "reflecting"
- "showcasing"
- "contributing to"
- "fostering"

Preferred correction:

- Break stacked participial phrases into explicit propositions.
- Replace pseudo-analysis with evidence, attribution, or a direct statement.

### 3. Promotional or brochure-like language

Watch for:

- "groundbreaking"
- "vibrant"
- "rich"
- "remarkable"
- "stunning"
- "renowned"

Preferred correction:

- Use descriptive, evidential wording rather than admiration.
- Prefer measurable or historically grounded detail.

### 4. Vague attributions

Watch for:

- "experts argue"
- "observers have noted"
- "industry reports suggest"
- "many believe"

Preferred correction:

- Name the study, report, dataset, or scholarly group where possible.
- If attribution cannot be made specific, rewrite the sentence more cautiously.

### 5. AI vocabulary clustering

Watch for repeated use of:

- "additionally"
- "crucial"
- "delve"
- "enhance"
- "foster"
- "garner"
- "highlight"
- "intricate"
- "landscape"
- "pivotal"
- "showcase"
- "testament"
- "underscore"
- "valuable"
- "vibrant"

Preferred correction:

- Replace abstract prestige words with simpler and more exact alternatives.
- Reuse the clearest word when repetition is more natural than synonym cycling.

### 6. Copula avoidance

Watch for:

- "serves as"
- "stands as"
- "functions as"
- "represents"
- "boasts"
- "features"

Preferred correction:

- Prefer `is`, `are`, `has`, or another simple verb when no added nuance is needed.

### 7. Rule-of-three padding

Watch for:

- triads added for polish rather than meaning
- repeated three-part slogan structures

Preferred correction:

- Keep only the items that materially matter.
- Use two items or four items when that is the real structure.

### 8. Negative parallelism

Watch for:

- "not only X, but also Y"
- "it is not just X; it is Y"
- "not merely ... but ..."

Preferred correction:

- State the point directly unless the contrast carries real argumentative weight.

### 9. Formulaic challenge-conclusion pattern

Watch for:

- "Despite these challenges ..."
- "Nevertheless, the field continues to thrive ..."
- "The future looks bright ..."

Preferred correction:

- Replace generic resilience language with specific limitations, responses, or implications.

### 10. Generic positive conclusions

Watch for:

- "the findings highlight the importance of" used without specificity
- "exciting avenues for future research"
- "this represents a major step forward"

Preferred correction:

- End with the actual contribution, limitation, or next research task.

## Structural Cleanup Rules

- Avoid stacking transitions in adjacent sentences.
- Avoid repeated sentence openings across a paragraph.
- Avoid inline-header bullet habits that read like chatbot formatting.
- Prefer sentence rhythm variation, but keep it controlled and readable.
- Use repetition when it clarifies reference; do not force elegant variation.

## Academic-Safe Humanization Pass

Before finalising a paragraph, check:

1. Is the claim more inflated than the evidence warrants?
2. Are there vague authorities where a specific citation should appear?
3. Are multiple `-ing` phrases pretending to do analytical work?
4. Is the paragraph ending in generic uplift rather than a real point?
5. Does the wording sound assembled from prestige terms rather than written from understanding?

If yes, revise toward specificity, directness, and proportion.
