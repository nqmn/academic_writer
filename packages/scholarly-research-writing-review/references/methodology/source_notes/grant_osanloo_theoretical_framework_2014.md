# Understanding, Selecting, and Integrating a Theoretical Framework in Dissertation Research: Creating the Blueprint for Your "House"

**Citation:** Grant, C., & Osanloo, A. (2014). Understanding, selecting, and integrating a theoretical framework in dissertation research: Creating the blueprint for your "house." *Administrative Issues Journal: Connecting Education, Practice, and Research, 4*(2), 12–26. DOI: 10.5929/2014.4.2.9

**Keywords:** Theoretical framework, dissertation, doctoral, academic writing, research methods

---

## Core Argument

The theoretical framework is the "blueprint" for the entire dissertation. Without it, the study lacks structure and vision. The framework shapes every decision in the research process — from topic selection and research questions to methodology and data analysis. The article uses a house-building analogy throughout.

---

## The Blueprint Analogy

| Blueprint Element | Dissertation Equivalent |
|-------------------|------------------------|
| **Elevation drawing** (exterior view, style) | Theoretical framework — the overall worldview/lens |
| **Floor plan** (interior layout, room flow) | Conceptual framework — how concepts flow and connect internally |
| **Electrical system** (connects all rooms) | The thread connecting problem, purpose, significance, RQs, methodology, and analysis |

> Just as a contractor cannot build without instructions, one cannot guide a reader through a dissertation without a clear theoretical framework.

---

## What Is a Theoretical Framework?

A theoretical framework is:
- The **blueprint** for the entire dissertation inquiry
- A structure that guides research by relying on a **formal, established, coherent theory**
- The selected theory (or theories) that undergirds the researcher's thinking on the topic
- The **researcher's lens** with which to view the world (Merriam, 2001)

**Key criteria** (Lovitts, 2005): must be appropriate, logically interpreted, well understood, and aligned with the research question.

The theoretical framework has **implications for every decision** in the research process (Mertens, 1998).

---

## Theoretical vs. Conceptual Framework

| | Theoretical Framework | Conceptual Framework |
|-|-----------------------|----------------------|
| **Source** | Derived from existing, tested theory in the literature | Researcher's own understanding of how the problem will be explored |
| **Nature** | Pre-existing, validated by others | Constructed by the researcher |
| **Timing** | Typically developed *a priori* (before data collection) | May emerge during data analysis (*a posteriori*) |
| **Analogy** | Elevation blueprint (exterior style) | Floor plan (interior layout) |
| **Definition** | Established, coherent explanation of phenomena and relationships | System of concepts, assumptions, and beliefs guiding the research plan (Miles & Huberman, 1994) |
| **Content** | Theorists, key theoretical principles | Key factors, constructs/variables, presumed relationships among them |

**Example (Table 1 in the paper):**
- Theoretical Framework: Educational Equity Theory (Marx, 1975; Brookover & Lezotte, 1981)
- Conceptual Framework: RtI (Response to Intervention) Best Practices — screening, diagnosis, progress monitoring

## Selecting an Appropriate Theoretical Framework

### 8-Step Process

1. Begin by identifying your beliefs (epistemological worldview)
2. Consider several theories that intersect with your epistemological values
3. Develop a working knowledge of those theories
4. Conduct a brief literature review to find theoretical support
5. Consult ProQuest Dissertations & Theses Database for prior applications
6. Consider arguments that oppose your beliefs and theories
7. Apply answers to "how" the theory connects to your problem, purpose, significance, and design
8. Select one theoretical framework providing a solid, descriptive "blueprint"

> If you view the world through a pragmatic lens, critical theory would not be the best fit. If through interconnectedness, social network theory might be appropriate.

---

## Key Components to Align with the Theoretical Framework

### Problem, Purpose, and Significance
- **Problem**: defines the root issue and interaction among variables; must connect to the theoretical framework
- **Purpose**: defines aims and outcomes; must explain how the theoretical framework relates
- **Significance**: describes the "so what"; must explain why the chosen theoretical framework matters

### Research Questions
- Must embody recognizable aspects of the theoretical framework
- The framework "connects the reader to existing knowledge" while RQs serve as liaisons between existing knowledge and the problem
- Example using educational equity theory: "How can school administrators encourage more active engagement by parents of high-needs students as a way to promote equal access?"

---

## Integrating the Theoretical Framework

### In the Literature Review
The theoretical framework guides the organization and content of the literature review. A strong dissertation literature review includes:

1. Brief statement of topic
2. Introduction to the organization of the literature review
3. Identification of the theoretical framework (theory definition, key theorists, history)
4. Specification of key theoretical principles organized around conceptual subheadings
5. Identification of conflicts and controversies in the literature
6. Identification of gaps in existing literature
7. In the last paragraph only: how the proposed study connects to existing literature

### Concept Mapping
A concept map helps align the theoretical framework and literature review visually:
- Write ideas in boxes linked with arrows
- Start general → funnel to specific
- Frame the entire map with the theoretical framework and research question

### In Research Methods
- The framework dictates the data collection plan and illuminates data
- Quantitative: framework structures instruments and variables
- Qualitative: framework may emerge *a posteriori* from data analysis
- Higher explicit integration of theory → stronger dissertation

---

## Checklist for Integrating a Theoretical Framework

1. Which discipline will the theory be applied to?
2. Is the theory an appropriate fit with the methodological plan?
3. Does the methodology draw from the principles, concepts, and tenets of the framework?
4. How big is the theory? Are specific concepts selected to meet study objectives?
5. Do the problem, purpose, and significance align well with the framework?
6. Can the theory be used with the research questions, or do RQs need modification?
7. How does the framework inform the literature review?
8. Does the data analysis plan use *a priori* codes from the framework, or *a posteriori* codes for a new framework?
9. Does the framework undergird conclusions, implications, and recommendations?

## Applied Example: "Alicia's" Study

- **Epistemological stance**: Critical theorist
- **Problem**: Gender gap in dissertation topic selection (school finance) in an Ed.D. program
- **Purpose**: Understand the gender gap; provide strategies to encourage female students in finance topics
- **Theoretical Framework**: Feminist Theory (key principles: gender discrimination, oppression, hegemony, patriarchy)
- **Conceptual Framework**: Gender socialization in education, women's professional experiences, male dominance in finance disciplines
- **Methods**: Qualitative case study — interviews, focus groups, reflective journaling, document analysis
- **Analysis**: Thematic analysis using *a priori* categories from feminist theory

---

## Key Takeaways

- The theoretical framework must be identified at the **inception** of dissertation work
- All research is theoretical — even the absence of an explicit framework reflects implicit assumptions
- Without theory, findings have "limited usefulness" (Sarter, 2005)
- Journal submissions are often rejected for being **atheoretical** (Silver & Herbst, cited in Lester, 2005)
- The framework should become so woven into the study that it becomes "indistinguishable from the house itself"
