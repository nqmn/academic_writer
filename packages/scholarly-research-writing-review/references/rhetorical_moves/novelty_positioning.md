# Novelty Positioning

## Contribution Dimensions

- Methodological novelty
- Architectural or system novelty
- Dataset or corpus novelty
- Analytical novelty
- Theoretical novelty
- Performance novelty
- Integration novelty

## Theoretical Novelty Levels (Lim, 2026)

From lowest to highest:

1. Generalizability — testing in a new context; adds replicability.
2. Adaptation — fitting to new context without core changes; adds contextualisation.
3. Modification — changing components; adds depth.
4. Extension — extending the theory; adds breadth.
5. New development — developing an entirely new theory; highest claim.

Always match the framing level to the actual evidence. Generalizability studies must not be framed as extensions.

## Theoretical Interestingness (Lim, 2026)

A novel contribution gains rhetorical traction when it is also interesting. Three forms:

- Counterintuitivity: the finding or framework is surprising but, on reflection, sensible.
- Paradoxicality: the co-existence of apparently opposing outcomes or mechanisms.
- Noteworthiness: a broader scope or deeper complexity than existing assumptions allow.

Signal interestingness through careful framing, not through hype. State what the reader would not have expected and why the expectation was reasonable.

## Positioning Rules

- Compare the contribution against a concrete baseline in prior work.
- Use modest formulations such as "extends", "refines", "provides new evidence", or "offers a more explicit account".
- Separate novelty from importance; a study may be valuable without being radically unprecedented.
- Name the dimension of novelty explicitly: theoretical, methodological, contextual, or integrative.

## Avoid

- "For the first time" unless strictly defensible.
- Inflated claims of disruption or transformation.
- Vague novelty language detached from the actual contribution.
- Claiming a higher novelty level than the evidence supports.
