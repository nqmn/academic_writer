# Contribution Framing

## What to Emphasise

- What the study adds
- Which gap it addresses (unexplored / underdeveloped / uncertain)
- How the addition changes understanding, method, or practice

## Typology of Theoretical Contributions (Lim, 2026)

Listed in ascending novelty order:

1. Theoretical generalizability — tests an existing theory in a new context or sample; adds reproducibility or replicability evidence.
2. Theoretical adaptation — fits an existing theory to a new context without substantially changing its core; adds contextualisation.
3. Theoretical modification — changes aspects or components to better fit a context; adds theoretical depth.
4. Theoretical extension — creates an extension to an existing theory; adds theoretical breadth.
5. New theoretical development — develops an entirely new theory; highest novelty level.

Use this hierarchy to calibrate how strongly a contribution claim should be framed. A replication study cannot be framed as if it were a new theoretical development.

## Strength of Theory Development (Lim, 2026)

Three pillars:

- Theoretical support: logical and evidence-based arguments underpinning assumptions.
- Theoretical legitimacy: empirical validation (theoretical soundness and granularity).
- Theoretical interestingness: counterintuitivity (unusual but sensible), paradoxicality (co-existence of opposing ends), or noteworthiness (broader scope or deeper complexity than existing assumptions).

## Framing Patterns

- "This study contributes to the literature by ..."
- "The findings extend current understanding of ..."
- "The analysis provides new evidence regarding ..."
- "The study offers a framework for ..."
- "Building on [theory], this study adapts the framework to ..."
- "The present work modifies [theoretical element] to account for ..."

## Theoretical Implications: The 3Rs (Lim, 2026)

When writing the implications of a theoretical contribution:

1. Recap — state the type of theory, what it explains or predicts, the contribution level, and the interestingness form.
2. Retrospect — revisit the theoretical gaps identified at the outset and explain how the contribution affirms, extends, refines, challenges, or refutes existing understanding.
3. Reimagine — identify new areas of inquiry, propose modifications or extensions, and suggest new contexts or integrations.

## Constraint

- Contributions must remain proportional to the evidence actually presented.
- Never claim a higher novelty level (e.g., new development) for work that qualifies only as adaptation or modification.
