# Gap Identification

## Gap Classification: The 3Us (Lim, 2026)

Three primary types of theoretical gap:

- Unexplored: the aspect has received no research attention — emerging topics, untested contexts, or overlooked populations.
- Underdeveloped: the aspect is recognised in the literature but remains theoretically or empirically thin.
- Uncertain: existing studies have produced conflicting or inconsistent findings that remain unresolved.

## Problematizing the Gap (Lim, 2026)

A gap without a problem has no meaning. A problem without a gap has no novelty.

Three forms of problematization:

- Debates: contested assumptions creating confusion (necessity) or opportunity (importance).
- Disconnects: gaps between theory and practice that highlight relevance.
- Direness: urgency when debates and disconnects have been repeatedly ignored.

## Acceptable Gap Types (extended)

- Empirical gap: the phenomenon has not been studied in a relevant setting or population.
- Methodological gap: previous work uses designs that limit inference, reproducibility, or generalisability.
- Theoretical gap: key concepts, mechanisms, or constructs remain under-specified or untested.
- Comparative gap: existing studies do not compare the cases, models, or conditions required for the current question.
- Context gap: findings may not transfer to an important setting, population, region, or application domain.
- Integration gap: relevant strands exist but have not been connected in a way that answers the present question.

## Writing Rules

- Anchor the gap in literature, not intuition.
- Classify the gap before writing: is it unexplored, underdeveloped, or uncertain?
- Pair the gap with a clear problem — necessity, importance, relevance, or urgency.
- Avoid saying "no study exists" unless the claim is verifiable.
- Pair the gap with why it matters for knowledge, practice, or method.

## Strong Formulation Pattern

1. Summarise what the literature has established.
2. Classify the remaining gap (unexplored / underdeveloped / uncertain).
3. State what remains unresolved and why the absence matters.
4. Explain the consequence for knowledge, practice, or method.
